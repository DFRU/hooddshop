# Legal Risk Assessment — Jersey-Inspired Hood Cover Redesign

**Date**: 2026-04-11
**Assessor**: AI-assisted (requires review by qualified legal professional)
**Matter**: Creating car hood cover designs closely replicating official FIFA World Cup 2026 national team jersey designs
**Privileged**: No — preliminary framework only; not legal advice

---

## 1. Risk Description

HOOD'D intends to redesign its car hood cover product line to closely match the official FIFA World Cup 2026 national team jerseys (home and away kits) sold on store.fifa.com, manufactured by Nike, Adidas, Puma, and Jordan. The stated goal is to get "as legally close as possible" so customers can "rep the same jersey they see on the field."

---

## 2. Background and Context

**The Products**: Sublimation-printed stretch car hood covers ($49.99 CAD). Not apparel — automotive accessories.

**The Reference Designs**: 115 products on the FIFA store, covering ~40 nations with home/away kits. Manufacturers include Nike (USA, Brazil, Canada, France, Portugal, Netherlands, England), Adidas (Mexico, Argentina, Germany, Spain, Belgium, Colombia, Italy, Japan, Scotland, Austria, Jamaica), Puma, and Jordan.

**Current State**: HOOD'D currently has 48 products in a "Jersey Line" with original designs inspired by national colors. The proposal is to redesign these to more closely match the actual 2026 kits.

---

## 3. Risk Analysis — Five Distinct IP Risks

### RISK A: Federation Crests & Logos
**Severity: 5 (Critical) × Likelihood: 5 (Almost Certain) = 25 — RED**

National football federation crests (US Soccer crest, FFF rooster, DFB eagle, CBF logo, etc.) are registered trademarks owned by each federation. Reproducing these on a commercial product without a license is trademark infringement and potentially counterfeiting.

**Rationale**: Federations actively enforce these marks. Applying them to commercial products is textbook infringement regardless of product category (car accessories vs. apparel). Likelihood is "almost certain" because if the crests are included, enforcement is inevitable from Nike/Adidas or the federation's licensing arm.

### RISK B: Manufacturer Logos (Nike Swoosh, Adidas 3-Stripes, Jordan Jumpman, Puma Cat)
**Severity: 5 (Critical) × Likelihood: 5 (Almost Certain) = 25 — RED**

These are among the most actively enforced trademarks in the world. Nike and Adidas maintain dedicated brand protection teams and file thousands of DMCA/trademark complaints annually.

**Rationale**: Including any manufacturer logo on an unlicensed product is counterfeiting. Criminal penalties possible. Shopify will remove products on first complaint.

### RISK C: FIFA / World Cup Branding
**Severity: 4 (High) × Likelihood: 5 (Almost Certain) = 20 — RED**

"FIFA World Cup 2026™", the tournament emblem, and "World Cup" are protected marks. Using these on products or in marketing constitutes trademark infringement.

**Rationale**: FIFA's Rights Protection Programme is one of the most aggressive IP enforcement operations in global sports. They monitor online marketplaces actively.

### RISK D: Specific Jersey Design Compositions (Copyright / Trade Dress)
**Severity: 4 (High) × Likelihood: 3 (Possible) = 12 — ORANGE**

The specific artistic arrangement of graphic elements on each jersey — the exact star pattern on USA Home, the precise stripe widths/angles on USA Away, the wave pattern on Japan — constitutes copyrightable artistic expression. The overall visual impression may also be protected as trade dress.

**Rationale**: This is the gray area. Copyright protects specific expression, not general ideas. A design that is "closely inspired by" but not a copy would need to be evaluated on a case-by-case basis. Severity is high because Nike/Adidas could pursue a claim. Likelihood is "possible" rather than "certain" because (a) the product category is different (car covers vs. apparel), (b) enforcement depends on how close the design actually is, and (c) copyright protection for functional garment designs has limits under Star Athletica v. Varsity Brands (US, 2017).

### RISK E: National Flag Colors & General Color Associations
**Severity: 1 (Negligible) × Likelihood: 1 (Remote) = 1 — GREEN**

National flag colors are public domain. No one owns "blue, white, and red for France" or "green and gold for Brazil." Using a country's traditional color palette on a product is not infringing.

**Rationale**: Colors in the abstract are not protectable. Only specific combinations applied in specific arrangements cross into protectable territory.

## 4. What IS and IS NOT Protectable — Summary

### SAFE TO USE (Not Protectable)
- National flag colors and color palettes
- Country names (e.g., "USA", "Brazil", "Germany")
- National flag imagery (public domain)
- General geometric patterns (stripes, chevrons, gradients) in original arrangements
- The concept of "a home kit" or "an away kit" color scheme
- Historical/cultural motifs associated with a nation

### MUST NOT USE (Protected)
- Any federation crest, logo, or badge
- Any manufacturer logo (Nike, Adidas, Puma, Jordan, etc.)
- "FIFA", "World Cup", FIFA emblem, or tournament marks
- The exact specific design composition copied from any jersey
- Player names, squad numbers, or trademarked typography
- Official kit product names or SKU identifiers

### GRAY ZONE (Case-by-Case)
- Design compositions that are "heavily inspired by" but not identical to official kits
- Specific pattern styles (USA's star repeat pattern, Japan's wave motif) reimagined in a different arrangement
- Color gradient directions and proportions that evoke a specific jersey

| Option | Risk Level | Effectiveness | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A. Color-palette only** | GREEN (1-4) | High safety, moderate appeal | Use only the country's flag colors in original geometric compositions. No reference to specific jersey designs. |
| **B. Inspired-by with original composition** | YELLOW (5-9) | Good balance | Use jersey color palette + general design mood (e.g., "dark with subtle star texture" for USA Home) but create completely original graphic compositions. Different proportions, different arrangements, different graphical elements. |
| **C. Close replica without logos** | ORANGE (10-15) | High appeal, high risk | Replicate the jersey design composition closely but omit all logos/crests/marks. Still risks copyright/trade dress claims. |
| **D. Direct copy with logos** | RED (16-25) | Maximum appeal, maximum risk | Counterfeiting. Do not do this. |

---

## 6. Recommended Approach

**Option B: "Inspired-by with original composition"** — YELLOW risk level

This means:
1. Match the jersey's PRIMARY and SECONDARY colors exactly
2. Reference the jersey's general design MOOD (e.g., "stars pattern" for USA, "wave pattern" for Japan, "classic stripes" for Argentina)
3. Create a COMPLETELY ORIGINAL graphic composition — different proportions, different element sizes, different spatial arrangement
4. Include the country name or flag (not the federation crest)
5. Use HOOD'D's own branding prominently
6. Avoid any text or imagery that references FIFA, World Cup, or any manufacturer
7. Describe products as "national team colors" or "country-inspired" — never as "replica" or "official"

**What this looks like in practice**:
- USA Home: Dark navy background with an original star pattern (different star size, spacing, and arrangement from the Nike design) + red/white accent elements
- USA Away: Red and white horizontal bands (different widths and proportions from Nike) + navy accents
- Brazil Home: Yellow base with green trim in an original layout
- Argentina Home: Light blue and white vertical elements (not identical stripe widths)

---

## 7. Residual Risk After Mitigation (Option B)

**Severity: 3 (Moderate) × Likelihood: 2 (Unlikely) = 6 — YELLOW**

With original compositions using only national colors and general mood, the risk drops substantially. A claim is possible but unlikely because: (a) the product category is different (automotive vs. apparel), (b) the designs are original compositions, (c) flag colors are unprotectable. The main residual risk is a trade dress argument if any specific design is too close to the original.

---

## 8. Monitoring Plan

- **Pre-launch**: Review each design against the official jersey to confirm sufficient differentiation. No design should be immediately recognizable as "that's the Nike/Adidas jersey."
- **Product listings**: Avoid any language suggesting official affiliation. Use "inspired by [country] national colors" not "replica" or "match kit."
- **Ongoing**: Monitor for takedown notices. Have a rapid response plan to modify or remove any design that receives a complaint.
- **Quarterly**: Re-assess as tournament approaches (June/July 2026) — enforcement typically intensifies during the event.

---

## 9. Critical Next Steps

1. **Do NOT include any federation crests, manufacturer logos, or FIFA marks** — Non-negotiable.
2. **Design each cover as an original composition** — Use the jersey as color/mood reference only, not as a template.
3. **Engage an IP attorney before launch** — This assessment is a framework, not legal advice. An attorney should review the final designs.
4. **Add a disclaimer to the site** — "HOOD'D is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or sponsored by FIFA, any national football federation, or any kit manufacturer."
5. **Product naming** — Use "HOOD'D | [Country] — [Design Line]" not anything referencing the official kit.
